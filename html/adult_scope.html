This is a guide for the adult who has a strong interest in astronomy and wants to get into it more actively. Presumably, this means that you want to get a nice telescope.

If you are planning on getting a telescope for a child, then I have a different guide for you. There is a fair amount of content overlap between these two guides, but the thrust is different. In this one, I assume that you have a reasonable expectation that your interest in astronomy will last longer than a month or two. I also assume that you are willing to spend more money than you would on a young child, and that you are willing to "work harder" at it. Kids need a lot of instant gratification; adults are better able to appreciate subtle beauty and longer-term satisfaction.

I am very much in love with astronomy, in spite of a rocky start. It's a wonderful hobby that can be both challenging and relaxing (not to mention educational). I would like to share some of this love, partly to get you enthused and started on the right track, but also to give you a peek at what amateur astronomy is really all about. If it really isn't for you, I hope that this guide will save you the frustration and money that an unused telescope would cause you.

Like many beginners, I started out with my telescope, a set of star charts, and a lot of enthusiasm. The scope came with an instruction manual which gave a list of the Messier objects (galaxies, nebulae and star clusters) that it claimed should be within reach of the scope. So, I set about calibrating the setting circles for the sky's coordinates, pointed the scope at the coordinates where one of the objects was supposed to be, and took a look.

Nothing.

Let's just say that it has taken me until now (over 6 months) to figure out how I should have gotten started.

I don't claim that the approach described here is the only, or even the best way to get started in astronomy. I'm sure that many amateurs would argue with many of my choices. However, I believe that starting out this way is one way to get a good start. If you're as lost as I was, give this a try.

Would I Make a Good Astronomer?

Many people are disappointed when they first get a telescope. They're used to seeing amazing color photographs of galaxies and nebulae. But these photos are taken with long exposures in very sensitive films. When looking through the eyepiece of even a HUGE telescope, you won't see anything like that.

In my opinion, you need to have three characteristics (some would say character flaws) in order to build a long-lasting love of astronomy:

  • Love of Science - learning about the universe.
  • Love of Technology - shopping for, buying, and using the equipment (telescopes, eyepieces, finders, filters, dew shields, ... whole catalogs full of stuff).
  • Love of Observing - beauty, peace, challenge.

Different people will have these ingredients in different proportions - I happen to have a great love of science and observing, and only a mild interest in the technology. But I believe that you need at least some of all three to be a happy astronomer.

If you don't mind my rambling a bit, you can see my longer treatment of this subject. After reading it, I hope you'll have a better idea of whether you will stick with it for a while.
